# STAR story bank — template

Interviews ask the same handful of behavioural questions ("tell me about a time you…"). Instead
of improvising — badly, under pressure — you build a small **bank of 5–8 polished stories** once,
tag each to the competencies it demonstrates, and reuse them. The `interview-prep` skill pulls
from this bank when it scaffolds a prep doc.

**STAR** = **S**ituation · **T**ask · **A**ction · **R**esult. Keep each story tight (you should
be able to tell it in ~2 minutes), lead with the result when you can, and make the *Action*
section unmistakably about what **you** did, not the team.

Copy the blank template below for each of your own stories. A fully worked (fictional) example
follows so you can see the bar.

---

## Blank template

```markdown
### <Short memorable title>

- **Competencies:** <e.g. stakeholder management, prioritisation, dealing with ambiguity>
- **Best for questions like:** <"tell me about a time you handled conflicting priorities">
- **Source:** <which role / project this is from>

**Situation** — <1–2 sentences of context: where, when, what was at stake>

**Task** — <what you specifically were responsible for / the problem you had to solve>

**Action** — <the concrete steps YOU took. This is the heart — be specific, use "I", show judgement>

**Result** — <the outcome, quantified if at all possible. What changed because of you?>

**If pushed / follow-ups:** <what you'd say if they dig: what you'd do differently, what you learned>
```

## Worked example (fictional — replace with your own)

### The peak-season backlog rescue

- **Competencies:** prioritisation under pressure, cross-team coordination, customer focus
- **Best for questions like:** "tell me about a time you handled a crisis / a heavy workload / conflicting priorities"
- **Source:** Customer Operations role at Northwind Trading

**Situation** — During the busiest trading week of the year, Northwind's support queue blew past
1,200 open tickets and response times slipped from hours to three days. Customers were escalating
publicly and the team was underwater.

**Task** — I owned the support queue's health for the region. My job was to get response times
back under control without simply telling everyone to work longer.

**Action** — I pulled the last two weeks of tickets and found ~40% were three repeat issues with
known fixes. I wrote three canned-response macros and a one-page triage rule so the team could
clear those on sight, then split the queue into "fast lane" (known issues) and "needs a human"
and assigned owners to each. I flagged the single product bug behind the biggest cluster to
engineering with the data attached, so it actually got prioritised.

**Result** — Within four days the backlog was under 300 and median response time was back under
six hours. The triage rules stuck around after peak and became the team's default. The bug fix
removed that ticket category entirely.

**If pushed / follow-ups:** What I'd do differently — I reacted to the backlog; next time I'd
watch the leading indicator (ticket *inflow* by category) so I catch the spike before it becomes
a backlog. What I learned: most "crises" are a few root causes wearing a big number.

## Tips

- **5–8 stories cover most interviews.** Aim for variety: a delivery-under-pressure one, a
  conflict/stakeholder one, a failure-and-what-you-learned one, a leadership/initiative one, a
  data/analysis one.
- **One story can answer many questions** — tag it with every competency it genuinely shows.
- **Quantify the Result** wherever you honestly can. "Cut response time from 3 days to 6 hours"
  beats "improved response times".
- **Keep them true.** The bank is a memory aid, not a fiction generator. Embellished stories fall
  apart under follow-up questions.
